Pablo Picasso

  • 1881 - Born in Málaga, Spain and was baptized Pablo Diego José Francisco de Paula Juan Nepomuceno María de los Remedios Cipriano de la Santísima Trinidad Ruiz y Picasso in the Catholic Church, though he later became an atheist.
  • 1888 - Began receiving instruction in drawing and oil painting from his father who was a painter that specialized in naturalistic depictions of animals.
  • 1895 - Admitted to advance classes at the Barcelona School of Fine Arts at 13 years of age.
  • 1897 - His father send him to Madrid's Real Academia de Bellas Artes de San Fernando, which was one of Spain's foremost art school. However he bristled at the formal instruction and left the school shortly after enrollment. He instead spend much of his time being inspired by the exhibits of other artists in the Prado Museum. It was during this time that the realism of his work began the show Symbolist influences.
  • 1900 - Makes his first trip to Paris, which was the art capitol of Europe at the time. There he met a journalist and poet name Max Jacob, who help Picasso learn the French language and its literature.
  • 1901-1904 - Picasso's Blue Period. This period is characterized by the use of blue and blue-green shades in his paintings with the occasional of other colors. These paintings were somber and often depicted gaunt mothers with children, prostitutes, and beggars.
  • 1904-1906 - Picasso's Rose Period. Paintings from this period have a lighter tone and are rendered in oranges and pinks. The subjects were often circus people, acrobats and harlequins. It was during this time that Picasso started attracting patrons and collectors took notice of his work.
  • 1907-1909 - Picasso's African-influenced Period. This period was influenced by the African artifacts he was at the ethnographic museum at Palais du Trocadéro in 1907.
  • 1909-1912 - Analytic Cubism. This is a style of painting that Picasso developed during this period with Georges Braque. This style uses monochrome brownish and neutral colors to deconstruct and depict objects based on their shapes.
  • 1912-1919 Synthetic Cubism. This period was a continued evolution of the cubism genre, in which paper fragments were incorporated into pieces. Though many of his friends and colleagues had to join the war effort during World War I, Picasso was able to continue painting.
  • 1919-1929 - Neoclassicism and surrealism. After World War I, the are art community in Europe embraced the "return to order" which was reaction to the war that caused artist to reject the avant-garde art for traditional styles. Picasso followed suit by producing neoclassical works. By the mid 1920's, Picasso was producing surrealist paintings that were informed by cubism.
  • 1930-1939 - Much of Picasso's work during this time featured the minotaur, which is a symbol he picked up from the surrealists as it is an image they often used. One of his most famous pieces from this time is Guernica, which depicts the German Bombing of Guernica during the Spain Civil War.
  • 1939-1949 - Picasso was in Paris during the Nazi occupation, and since his work did not fit there ideal notion of art he was unable to put on exhibits during the war. However, he continued to work. He even cast bronze with materials smuggled to him by the French Resistance.
  • 1949-1973 - During these final years Picasso continued to evolve his expression through art. He reimagined works of great masters, produced a 50-foot public sculpture in Chicago, and made a few film appearances.
  • 1973 - Dies in Mougins, France at the age of 92.
